Summary of Hamiltonian formulation of Wilson's LGT

Summary of Hamiltonian Formulation of Wilson’s Lattice Gauge Theory

1. Hamiltonian from the Continuous Theory

From the continuous theory we have:

$$L = \int d^3x \left[ \frac{1}{2g^2} \text{tr}(F_{0i}^2) - \frac{1}{2g^2} \text{tr}(F_{ij}^2) \right] \tag{1.1}$$ Note that:

$$\text{tr}F_{0i}^2 \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{2a^4}} \text{tr}(2 - W_{i0} - W_{i0}^\dagger) = \frac{1}{a^2} \text{tr}(U_i^\dagger U_i) \tag{1.2}$$ $$\text{tr}F_{ij}^2 = \frac{1}{a^2} \text{tr}(2 - W_{ij} - W_{ij}^\dagger) \tag{1.3}$$ We have the discrete version of (1.1) by considering (1.2), (1.3) and ∫d3x → ∑xa3:

$$L = \sum_{x,i} \left[ \frac{a}{\sqrt{2a^4}} \text{tr}(U_i^\dagger(x) U_i(x)) - \sum_{\text{plaquettes}} \frac{1}{2g^2 a} \text{tr}(2 - W_P - W_P^\dagger) \right] \tag{1.4}$$ Our canonical coordinate is $ U_i(x) $. Consider an infinitesimal transform:

Ui(x) → (1+iϵ(x)Ta)Ui(x),  Ui†(x) → Ui†(x)(1−iϵ(x)Ta)

Momentum is the generator of differentiation of the coordinate, which in this case is the gauge generator:

$$E_{L,i}^a(x) = -i \frac{2a}{g^2} \text{tr}(\dot U_i(x) U_i^\dagger(x) T^a) \tag{1.6}$$ Where the subscript “L” denotes the “Left action”.

Also we can consider the Right Action:

Ui(x) → Ui(x)(1−iϵ(x)Ta) Right Electric Field:

$$E_{R,i}^a(x) = i \frac{2a}{g^2} \text{tr}(U_i^\dagger(x) \dot U_i(x) T^a) \tag{1.7}$$ It is easy to check that:

$$\sum_a E_{L,i}^a(x) E_{L,i}^a(x) = \sum_a E_{R,i}^a(x) E_{R,i}^a(x) = \frac{2a^2}{g^4} \text{tr}(\dot U_i^\dagger \dot U_i(x)) \tag{1.8}$$

Eliminate $ U^U $ using (1.8) to get the lattice Hamiltonian:

$$H = \frac{1}{a} \left( \sum_{x,i} E_{L,i}^a(x) E_{L,i}^a(x) + \frac{1}{g^2} \sum_{\text{plaquettes}} \text{tr}(2 - W_p - W_p^\dagger) \right)$$ Or, more explicitly:

$$H = \frac{1}{a} \left( g^2 \sum_{x,i} \text{tr}(E_{L,i}(x) E_{L,i}(x)) + \frac{1}{g^2} \sum_{\text{plaquettes}} \text{tr}(2 - W_p - W_p^\dagger) \right) \tag{1.9}$$ where $ E E^a T^a $.

2. Quantization

In the coordinate representation $ U | $, we have:

$$\hat{E}_{L,i}^a(x) = (T^a U_i(x))_{mn} \frac{\partial}{\partial (U_i(x))_{mn}} \tag{2.1}$$

[ÊL, ia(x),Ûj(y)] = TaUi(x)δxyδij

[ÊL, ia(x),ÊL, jb(y)] =  − ifabcEL, ic(x)δxyδij

Similarly for $ _{R,i}^a $:

[ÊR, ia(x),Ûj(y)] =  − Ûj(x)Taδxyδij

[ÊR, ia(x),ÊR, jb(y)] =  − ifabcER, ic(x)δxyδij

The Hamiltonian operator is:

$$\hat{H} = \frac{1}{a} \left\{ g^2 \sum_{x,i} \text{tr}(\hat{E}_{L,i}(x) \hat{E}_{L,i}(x)) + \frac{1}{g^2} \sum_{\text{plaquettes}} \text{tr}(2 - W_p - W_p^\dagger) \right\}$$

Where “trace” means tracing over the fundamental indices.

3. Classical and Quantum Relations between ( E_L ) and ( E_R )

3.1 Classical Case

$$E_{L,i}^a = -i \frac{2a}{g^2} \text{tr}(\dot U_i U_i^\dagger T^a), \quad E_{R,i}^a = i \frac{2a}{g^2} \text{tr}(U_i^\dagger \dot U_i T^a) \tag{3.1.1}$$

In Lie algebra form:

$$E_{L,i}(x) = -i \frac{a}{g^2} \dot U_i(x) U_i^\dagger(x), \quad E_{R,i}(x) = i \frac{2a}{g^2} U_i^\dagger(x) \dot U_i(x) \tag{3.1.2}$$

Relation between $ E_L $ and $ E_R $:

ER, i(x) =  − Ui(x)EL, i(x)Ui†(x)

Or in components:

ER, ia(x) =  − 2 tr(TaUi†(x)TbUi(x))EL, ib(x)

3.2 Quantum Case (2-site example)

For simplicity, let’s consider a 2-site system.

Let $ U $ denote the link from site 1 to site 2. E1 denotes the left E at site 1 and E2 denotes the right E at site 2.

Basic commutators:

[E1a,U] = TaU,  [E2a,U] =  − UTa

In coordinate representation:

E1a(U)mn = (TaU)mn,  E2a(U)mn =  − (UTa)mn

Then:

$$\overline{E}_2^a (U_{mn}) = - \left( U T^a U^\dagger \right)_{mn} i U_{kn}$$

 =  − 2TijbTmlb(UTaU†)jiUkn

 =  − 2(TbbU)ijTjkaUkr†TmlbUkn

 =  − 2tr(TbbUTaU†)TmlbUkn

 =  − 2tr(TbbUTaU†)Elb(Umn)

 ⇒ E2a =  − 2tr(UTaU†Tbb)Elb  (3,2,4)

It’s more logical to review the above derivation in reverse order.

Alternatively, by definition of E1 and E2:

E2 =  − U†E1U + TaTa

This differs from the classical case (3.1.3) by an extra term $ T^a T^a $ due to non-commutativity.

One can check that in quantum case, tr(U†E1U) = TaTa, and this preserves the tracesless property of E2: trE2 = 0

5.Symmetries: B_d and Charge Conjugation

Reflection: U → U†, E → ? Start with the commutation relation [Ea,U] = TaU $$\begin{array}{c} \xrightarrow{reflection}[E_i^{'a}, U^{\dagger}] = T^aU^{\dagger} \\ \Rightarrow [E_i^{'a}, U] = -UT^a \end{array}$$

this implies that $ E_i^{’a} = E_2^a $. Therefore, Reflection: U → U†, Eia → E2a, E2a → E1a  (5.1) Change conjugation Ai →  − (Ai)TU = eiaA → e−iaAT = (U†)T Here “T” means the transpose of fundamental indices. $$\xrightarrow{C} \quad U_{ij} \rightarrow U_{ji}^\dagger$$For $ E $: [Ea,U] = TaU† $$\xrightarrow{C}[CE_i^{a}C^{-1}, (U^{\dagger})^T] = -(T^a)^{T}(U^{\dagger})^T$$

$$\begin{array}{c} \xrightarrow{T}[CE_i^{a}C^{-1}, U^{\dagger}] = -U^\dagger T^a \\ \xrightarrow{\dagger}[CE_i^{a}C^{-1}, U] = T^aU \end{array}$$

This implies CE1aC−1 = E1a, and E1 = E1aTa →  − E1a(Ta)T =  − E1T. Thus $$E_{1ij}\xrightarrow{C}-E_{1ji} \tag{5.2}$$ (5.1) implies that whether E is Left or Right is more relavent to its spatial orientation.

EL lives on the starting end of the link and points to the ending end. ER lives on the ending end and points to the opposite direction.

(5.2) implies the sign of E revels the charge of E. Charge conjugation will reverse the loop and make the charge of E opposite. Note: Sections 4 (Schwinger–Dyson Equation) and parts of 5 (Symmetry) are incomplete in the original document.
